A Brief History of Allotments

The allotment system that we have today dates back to the 19th century.

In 1908, the Small Holdings and Allotment Act was introduced, meaning that local authorities had to provide allotments.

The Dig for Victory campaign during the 1940s encouraged people to use allotments. There were over 1.5 million across the UK.

Allotments were made available to everyone at the end of the first world war as a way of assisting returning service men.

The 1970s saw a spike in demand, most likely fuelled by trade union unrest and was depicted in the BBC television show The Good Life.

Animals Allowed

Allotment users are allowed to keep hens and rabbits, providing that they are not used for business or profit.

The average family would require 2-3 hens, as they can each lay up to five eggs per week.

Cockerels are not usually permitted due to the noise they make.

The keeping of bees, pigs, goats, and other livestock is subject to the landlord’s permission and is often included in the tenancy agreement.

Bees play a critical role in the pollination of plants, especially fruit crops. Having hives near an allotment can create tighter yields and better quality produce.

Third most popular pet in the UK are rabbits and can live up to 12 years. They need to be checked daily and exercised regularly.

Any animal that is thought to be a health hazard or nuisance can be removed, as can those that are at risk of harm or neglect.

Waiting List

In 2013, there were 78,827 people in English local authorities on allotment waiting lists.

Why are Allotments so Popular?

  1. Food grown on allotments means fewer food miles
  2. Local and national government reporting the benefits of allotments
  3. Increased focus on healthy eating in the media and government reports
  4. Higher interests in green energy and reducing carbon emissions
